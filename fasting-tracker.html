Body Stages — Physiology & Danger Flags
🚫 Obvious — Anything With Calories
🍎
Any food, snack, or meal
Calories trigger insulin release and immediately halt both ketosis and autophagy. The fast ends the moment caloric food enters digestion.
🧃
Juice, soda, sweetened drinks
Liquid sugar causes a rapid, large insulin spike — often worse than solid food because there's no fiber to slow absorption.
🍺
Alcohol
Alcohol is caloric (7 kcal/gram) and disrupts liver ketone production. Even "zero sugar" spirits impair the liver's fasting-state function.
🥛
Milk, cream, oat milk, almond milk
All contain calories — carbs, protein, or fat — in amounts that trigger insulin. Even a small splash of cream in coffee slows autophagy.
⚠️ Sneaky — Hidden Calories & Insulin Triggers
💧
Flavored sparkling water with "natural flavors"
Fruit oils and flavor compounds can trigger a cephalic phase insulin response — your body senses sweetness and releases a pre-emptive insulin burst even without sugar.
🍬
Gum and mints (even sugar-free)
Chewing and detecting sweetness signals your digestive system that food is incoming. Some gums also contain sorbitol, a caloric sugar alcohol.
💊
Some medications with fillers
Coated tablets and gummies may contain starch, sugar, or glycerin. Pure capsule medications are generally fine.
⚡
Flavored electrolytes with sugar
Sports drinks and many electrolyte packets contain sucrose, dextrose, or maltodextrin. Always read the ingredients list.
🦴
Bone broth (significant protein)
Amino acids activate mTOR (a cellular growth pathway) and suppress autophagy. Protein — even in liquid form — is still protein.
🧪 Supplements That May Break a Fast
💪
BCAAs
Amino acids activate mTOR and suppress autophagy. They also provide small calories. BCAAs break a fast for all practical purposes.
🥤
Protein powder
Any protein triggers an insulin and mTOR response — even unflavored varieties.
🍊
Gummy vitamins and some capsule fillers
Gummy vitamins contain sugar. Some capsule vitamins use starch or oil-based carriers. Pure powder supplements in plain capsules are generally fine.
🐟
Fish oil capsules
Pure fish oil is caloric. A single capsule is likely negligible for most fasting goals, but technically breaks a strict fast.
✅ Probably Fine — Common Worries That Aren't
☕
Black coffee
Zero calories, no measurable insulin response. May actually enhance autophagy. Don't add anything to it.
🍵
Plain tea — but read the label on "herbal"
Black, green, white, and oolong teas are fast-safe. Many herbal teas contain fruit pieces, fruit flavoring, or fruit oils — even when the packaging looks plain. These can carry natural sugars and trigger an insulin response. "Herbal" and "natural" do not mean sugar-free.
🫧
Plain sparkling water
Unflavored carbonated water is fine. Once "natural flavors" or fruit appear on the label, see Sneaky section above.
🧂
Salt / sodium
Zero calories, no insulin response. As insulin drops, the kidneys excrete sodium faster — salt intake becomes genuinely necessary in extended fasts.
⚡
Clean-label electrolyte powders
Sodium, potassium, magnesium, and stevia are generally fast-safe. Avoid anything listing maltodextrin, dextrose, or natural flavors.
💊
Most prescription medications
Most active pharmaceutical ingredients don't break a fast in a meaningful metabolic way. Always follow your prescriber's instructions.
